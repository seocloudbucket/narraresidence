How do I know if a property in Singapore is a good deal?

A good deal is not the cheapest listing—it’s the best combination of price, layout, location, and exit story. The framework below helps you score any property against the factors that actually drive return and livability. 1) Price vs. value: read the numbers in context

Start with district and project medians (psf and total quantum). Then normalise for like‑for‑like comparisons: floor, facing, renovation, and size band (e.g., 2‑bed vs 3‑bed). A unit 3–5% below the adjusted median can be attractive—if its layout and micro‑location are strong.

Quick valuation tools

  • Price‑per‑square‑foot (psf) vs project median
  • Effective psf after removing unusable spaces/corridors
  • Days‑on‑market and recent caveats for momentum

Rental sanity check

  • Compute gross yield: annual rent ÷ price
  • Compute net yield: subtract realistic expenses
  • Compare yields to nearby substitutes (same size/age)

2) Micro‑location: door‑to‑door reality beats district labels

Measure minutes to MRT, bus, and key amenities at a normal walking pace. Check the walking route: shelter, crossings, lighting. Map noise sources (expressways, schools) and sun path (harsh west sun). A strong micro‑location lifts rentability and exit price even within average districts.

3) Layout efficiency: the hidden driver of value

Layout determines how big a home feels and how quickly it rents/sells. Avoid long corridors and awkward angles. Prioritise squarish rooms, storage, cross‑ventilation, and natural light. Benchmark against the planning logic seen in Narra Residences Floor Plans—efficient units enjoy wider buyer pools.

4) Management health & future costs

Review MCST minutes, sinking fund balance, pending façade/lift works, and the maintenance fee trajectory. Well‑run estates protect your net yield and preserve resale value. Over‑amenitised projects with thin funds can spring special levies later.

5) The exit story: who buys your unit later—and why?

Define your future buyer. For compact units: singles and couples who want transit, lifestyle, and efficient spaces. For family layouts: schools, parks, storage, and privacy matter. A clear buyer story reduces days‑on‑market and discounting at resale.

Good‑deal checklist (10 points)

  1. Entry quantum aligns with your IPA and buffer.
  2. Adjusted psf at or below median for similar stacks.
  3. 5–8 minute walk to MRT/bus; safe, sheltered routing.
  4. Daily needs (groceries, clinics, eateries) within 500–800 m.
  5. Efficient floor plan; minimal corridor loss; logical furniture walls.
  6. Healthy MCST finances; no major special levies looming.
  7. Realistic net yield ≥ 3% for resilience.
  8. Favourable sun/wind orientation; limited noise exposure.
  9. Neighbourhood supply pipeline won’t flood your segment.
  10. Clear exit narrative with broad buyer/tenant pool.

7) Run the cash math (keep it conservative)

Compute net yield, include vacancy and routine capex, and stress‑test interest rates. If the deal only works with optimistic rent or razor‑thin costs, it’s not a good deal.

  • Gross yield = annual rent ÷ purchase price
  • Net yield = (annual rent − expenses) ÷ purchase price
  • CoC = annual pre‑tax cash flow ÷ total cash invested

8) Red flags that turn “cheap” into costly

  • Stacks with severe afternoon sun or persistent road/rail noise
  • Major upcoming supply right next door
  • Thin sinking fund or unresolved façade/waterproofing issues
  • Awkward layouts that shrink your buyer pool

FAQs

Is a low psf always a good deal?

No. Low psf can mask inefficient layouts, poor facing, or weak micro‑locations. Focus on total quantum and livability.

How close to MRT is “close enough”?

Generally 500–700 metres with a safe, sheltered walking route. Door‑to‑door time matters more than straight‑line distance.

Do facilities affect value?

Yes—but only if residents actually use them and MCST fees stay sensible. Practical gyms, pavilions, and co‑work nooks beat flashy, costly showpieces.
